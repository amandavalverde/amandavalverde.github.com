Amanda Valverde

10 March 2021

Amir Dori brings over a decade of experience and insights as a game designer to the article "Game Design UX Best Practices". While some of the concepts he shares are familiar to Design students who have studied UI/UX prior to reading the article, the concept of designing for monetization was particularly interesting to me. Placing interactive monetization elements in the comfort-to-reach areas on device screens increases the chance of users inadvertently interacting with them. This is known as dark-ux, and for better or worse it maximizes revenues. A pro-tip Dori mentions later in the article suggests disabling the close option via tapping the dim background of a store pop-up.

One piece of advice that I will be aware of in designing web content that relates to usability in overlays is avoiding the X in pop-ups:

Many pop-ups contain a default design choice of an “X” button to close them. Most users recognize this “X” button as a pattern of an ‘annoying’ content and closing the pop-up instantly. If your pop-up contains valuable content to the users, and you’d like to increase the probability that they’ll read this content, make sure that the “Close” button will be designed as one of the user’s choices and not as an “X” button.

Additional notes from this section highlighted redundancy in design that is ineffective. Duplicate options that carry the same value or meaning should be avoided. I actually encountered this issue in my overlays in the User Testing assignment; my users quickly pointed out that the overlays in my prototype did not need an X and a close button, especially since they were more likely to seek out the X because it is a ubiquitous GUI element.

The focus of Michael Gonchar's article entitled "10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills" is best encapsulated in this quote:

Closely reading any text, whether written or visual, requires that students proceed more slowly and methodically, noticing details, making connections and asking questions. This takes practice. But it certainly helps when students want to read the text.

Using images to create visual interest, heighten awareness, and improve discernment is tremendously useful in the effort to educate an audience, particularly students who can then draw parallels between images and the text it is nested in. 26 January 2021

Naema Baskanderi's article from UX Planet entitled "Best Practices for Modals / Overlays / Dialog Windows" illustrates how overlays can be implemented in effective UI design. Unfortunately, overlays are often overutilized, prompting users to instinctively and automatically close them from the onset; Baskanderi likens them to the universally-reviled pop-up windows of internet past.

Overlays, also referred to as modals or dialog windows, are suggested for grabbing the user’s attention, obtaining user input (e.g., sign up or login), and showing additional information (both in and out of context). The anatomy of an overlay should also be carefully crafted. Elements such as an escape hatch (cancel, close, click outside window, escape key), descriptive title, and button labels with actionable, understandable names should enhance usability with adequate mapping and discernable affordances. Baskanderi encourages designers to place modals toward the upper portion of the page, but cautions against making them larger than 50% of the screen in order to preserve context. The lightbox effect, which I have been interested in using in future projects for the course, is also useful for drawing focus to the overlay and indicating that the user cannot interact with the parent page. Finally, the author urges us to make dialog windows user-initiated to avoid having them dismissed rapidly as an uninvited pop-up.

Modals are discouraged for use on mobile devices as they can often be too large or too small when translated for smaller screens. There are also some key practices that will make using overlays easier and more intuitive for users with accessibility concerns:

  • The element which triggers the dialog must be keyboard accessible
  • Once the focus is moved into the dialog, it should be “trapped” inside it until the dialog is closed.
  • Each overlay window must have a keyboard accessible control to close that window.

Baskanderi also briefly mentions ARIA, Accessible Rich Internet Applications (ARIA) defines ways to make Web content and Web applications more accessible. For more information you can watch ARIA HTML Tutorial - What is ARIA & Why it's Important to Use! on YouTube.

21 January 2021

The principles from Salim Ansari's "Best practices for form design" were useful in mitigating a number of frustrations that myriad users, myself included, have encountered with forms. One of the more interesting aspects of the article was Ansari's mention of cognitive psychology in UX design, specifically the concept of cognitive load. Ansari writes of multiple steps: Studies shows that providing a clear picture of the number of steps it takes to complete the process significantly increases form completion. From the physiological point of view, if users complete the first step, they are more likely to complete the form. Always provide a clear picture of the number of steps left or the percentage of completion through a progress bar.

However, what piqued my interest most were the best practices for accessibility. In the pursuit of stylized web design I think it is easy to forget that some users rely on screen readers, have visual impairments, have cognitive disabilities, etc. These users may also benefit from varied approaches to feedback and responsiveness that we have not already considered. I think an assignment based on designing for accessibility could be really enlightening for us as burgeoning designers. As someone who has never used a screen reader or other assistive technologies, exposure to these kinds of devices and interventions might help us design with greater empathy. I would have loved to do a design internship in a school or facility where I could interact with people who rely on these resources in order to learn and communicate.
